## Overview

Oat milk is produced by blending whole oats or oat groats with water, then straining the mixture to remove the solid fiber. Enzymes are often added during production to break down some of the oat starch into simpler sugars, which gives commercial oat milk its characteristic mild sweetness without added sugar. Originating from Swedish food scientist Rickard Öste's research in the early 1990s, oat milk has grown into the most popular plant-based milk alternative globally, favored for its creamy consistency, neutral flavor, and significantly lower environmental footprint compared to dairy and other plant milks.

## Nutritional Highlights

- **Beta-glucan fiber** — Oat milk retains a portion of oats' soluble beta-glucan, which has clinically proven cholesterol-lowering effects and helps moderate post-meal blood sugar spikes.
- **Carbohydrates** — Oat milk is higher in carbohydrates than most nut milks (around 16 g per 240 ml serving), providing useful energy for active individuals.
- **Fortified calcium** — Most commercial oat milks are fortified with calcium carbonate to deliver 350–400 mg per serving, comparable to dairy milk.
- **Fortified vitamin D** — Added vitamin D helps support calcium absorption and bone health, particularly important for those avoiding dairy.
- **Vitamin B12** — Many brands fortify with B12, making fortified oat milk a practical source for vegans and vegetarians.
- **Riboflavin (B2)** — Often added during fortification; supports energy metabolism and cellular growth.

## Health Benefits

The beta-glucan content in oat milk, while lower than in whole oats, is sufficient to have a meaningful impact on cardiovascular health when consumed regularly. Beta-glucan forms a viscous gel in the digestive tract that traps bile acids and cholesterol, preventing their reabsorption and prompting the liver to pull more LDL cholesterol from the bloodstream. The FDA has approved a health claim for oat beta-glucan and heart disease risk reduction — a status awarded to very few food components.

Oat milk's moderate glycemic index is partly managed by its beta-glucan content, which slows glucose absorption. Choosing unsweetened versions further reduces the glycemic impact. For people with dairy allergies or lactose intolerance, fortified oat milk offers a nutritionally comparable alternative for meeting calcium and vitamin D needs without digestive discomfort.

From an environmental standpoint, oat milk requires significantly less land and water to produce than dairy milk and generates a fraction of the greenhouse gas emissions. It also requires less water than almond milk, which is grown in drought-prone regions. For health-conscious consumers who also consider environmental impact, oat milk consistently ranks as one of the most sustainable choices.

Oat milk's lack of nuts makes it safe for people with tree nut allergies, and it is naturally free of soy. For people with celiac disease or gluten sensitivity, certified gluten-free oat milk is available and safe to consume.

## How to Use

- **Choose unsweetened varieties** — Flavored and sweetened oat milks can contain 12–16 g of added sugar per serving. Unsweetened versions are preferable for everyday use in coffee, cereal, and cooking.
- **Pick barista editions for frothing** — Barista-formulated oat milks contain added oil and stabilizers that allow the milk to steam and froth without curdling, making them ideal for lattes and cappuccinos.
- **Use in baking and cooking** — Oat milk substitutes one-to-one for dairy milk in most baking recipes. Its mild flavor doesn't compete with other ingredients, and its slight viscosity can add body to soups and sauces.
- **Shake or stir before using** — Separation is natural in oat milk; a quick shake or stir before pouring restores a uniform consistency.
- **Store properly** — Unopened shelf-stable oat milk keeps at room temperature for months. Once opened, refrigerate and use within 7–10 days.
