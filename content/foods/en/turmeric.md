## Overview

Turmeric (Curcuma longa) is a rhizomatous plant of the ginger family native to the Indian subcontinent and Southeast Asia, where it has been cultivated for over 4,000 years as a spice, dye, and medicine. The bright orange-yellow root is dried and ground into the familiar golden powder that colours South Asian curries, Middle Eastern rice dishes, and the traditional Ayurvedic drink "golden milk." India produces and consumes approximately 80% of the world's turmeric supply, and the spice remains central to Ayurvedic and Unani medical traditions. Turmeric's principal bioactive compound is curcumin, which together with related curcuminoids makes up 2–5% of the dried powder by weight and has become one of the most intensively studied plant compounds in modern nutritional science.

## Nutritional Highlights

- **Curcumin (and curcuminoids):** The primary polyphenol in turmeric, curcumin inhibits NF-κB — a master regulator of inflammatory gene expression — and scavenges multiple free radical species. It is the subject of over 3,000 published studies examining effects on inflammation, cancer, neurodegeneration, and metabolic disease.
- **Manganese:** Turmeric is exceptionally rich in manganese, providing around 7.8 mg per teaspoon of dried powder — substantially above the adult daily adequate intake — supporting antioxidant enzymes and bone formation.
- **Iron:** Dried turmeric contains around 41 mg of iron per 100 g, though the amounts consumed as a spice are small. Nevertheless, regular culinary use contributes meaningfully to dietary iron.
- **Volatile oils (turmerones):** The essential oil fraction of turmeric contains ar-turmerone and related compounds with emerging evidence for neuroprotective and anti-cancer activity independent of curcumin.
- **Fibre:** Ground turmeric provides dietary fibre that supports gut motility and feeds beneficial intestinal bacteria.

## Health Benefits

Curcumin is one of the most well-validated natural anti-inflammatory agents in clinical research. It inhibits multiple molecular targets in the inflammatory cascade — including COX-2, LOX, NF-κB, and TNF-alpha — which collectively mediate chronic low-grade inflammation implicated in heart disease, type 2 diabetes, Alzheimer's disease, and certain cancers. A 2017 systematic review of 15 randomised controlled trials concluded that curcumin supplementation significantly reduced circulating levels of CRP and IL-6, two of the most widely used clinical markers of systemic inflammation.

Joint health is one of the most clinically studied applications of curcumin. A 2014 randomised trial comparing curcumin extract (500 mg twice daily) to ibuprofen (400 mg twice daily) in patients with knee osteoarthritis found equivalent improvements in pain and functional scores, with curcumin showing a superior safety profile and fewer gastrointestinal side effects. Multiple subsequent meta-analyses have confirmed that curcumin supplementation meaningfully reduces joint pain and stiffness in osteoarthritis patients.

Emerging neuroscience research supports turmeric's potential role in brain health. Curcumin crosses the blood-brain barrier and has been shown in animal studies and small human trials to reduce amyloid-beta plaque formation — a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease — and to increase levels of BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), a protein that promotes neuron survival and growth. A 2018 UCLA study found that daily curcumin supplementation for 18 months improved memory and attention in middle-aged adults and reduced amyloid and tau accumulation on PET brain imaging.

Curcumin has very poor natural bioavailability — it is rapidly metabolised and excreted. However, co-consuming turmeric with black pepper (which contains piperine) increases curcumin absorption by up to 2,000% by inhibiting the enzymes that break it down. Consuming turmeric with fat (such as in a curry with coconut milk or ghee) also improves absorption, as curcumin is fat-soluble.

## How to Use

- **Always pair with black pepper:** Even a small pinch of freshly cracked black pepper alongside turmeric dramatically increases curcumin bioavailability. This is the single most important preparation tip for maximising turmeric's health effects.
- **Cook into curries and rice dishes:** Turmeric is a staple in Indian dal, curry pastes, and biryani. Adding half a teaspoon to any stew, soup, or grain dish provides colour, a subtle earthy flavour, and meaningful curcumin along with the fat in the dish that aids absorption.
- **Make golden milk (turmeric latte):** Warm one cup of milk (or plant milk) with half a teaspoon of turmeric, a pinch of black pepper, a quarter teaspoon of cinnamon, and a small piece of fresh ginger. Sweeten with honey if desired. This traditional beverage is an effective, enjoyable way to consume turmeric daily.
- **Use fresh turmeric root:** Fresh turmeric root, available in Asian grocery stores, has a brighter, slightly more aromatic flavour than dried powder. Peel and grate it into stir-fries, juices, and salad dressings. Wear gloves — the pigment stains skin and clothing intensely.
- **Consider curcumin supplements for therapeutic use:** If using turmeric for a specific health goal such as joint support, dietary amounts may be insufficient. Look for supplements containing a standardised curcumin extract (such as BCM-95 or Meriva) that enhance bioavailability, and consult a healthcare provider before starting, particularly if taking blood thinners.
