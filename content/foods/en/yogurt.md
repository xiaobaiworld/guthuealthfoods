## Overview

Yogurt is produced by fermenting milk with specific bacterial cultures, principally Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus and Streptococcus thermophilus, which convert lactose into lactic acid, causing the milk to thicken and develop its characteristic tangy flavour. Yogurt-making has been practised for at least 8,000 years in the Middle East and Central Asia, and yogurt remains a dietary cornerstone across South Asian, Eastern European, Middle Eastern, and Mediterranean cuisines. Modern varieties include plain and flavoured yogurts, Greek-style (strained) yogurt, skyr (Icelandic strained yogurt), labneh (heavily strained yogurt cheese), kefir-style yogurts, and plant-based alternatives. The fermentation process transforms milk into a more nutritionally potent food — improving protein digestibility, reducing lactose content, increasing B vitamin bioavailability, and populating the product with live bacteria that confer gut microbiome benefits.

## Nutritional Highlights

- **Live probiotic cultures (Lactobacillus and Streptococcus species):** High-quality yogurt contains 100 million to 10 billion colony-forming units (CFU) per gram of live bacteria. These interact with the gut microbiome to improve microbial diversity, support the intestinal barrier, and modulate immune responses.
- **Complete protein:** Plain whole-milk yogurt provides around 9 g of protein per 170 g serving; Greek yogurt provides 15–20 g per serving after straining removes much of the whey. Yogurt proteins are highly digestible and include all essential amino acids.
- **Calcium:** A 170 g serving of yogurt provides approximately 200–300 mg of calcium — one-quarter to one-third of the adult daily requirement — in a highly bioavailable form, as the lactic acid in yogurt improves calcium absorption compared to unfermented milk.
- **Vitamin B12 and riboflavin (B2):** Yogurt is an excellent source of both vitamins, supporting red blood cell formation, neurological function, and cellular energy metabolism.
- **Potassium and phosphorus:** These minerals support blood pressure regulation, kidney function, and bone mineralisation, and are present in meaningful amounts in a standard serving of yogurt.
- **Conjugated linoleic acid (CLA):** Full-fat dairy yogurt, particularly from grass-fed cows, contains CLA, a fatty acid with evidence for anti-inflammatory and body composition-supporting effects in controlled trials.

## Health Benefits

The evidence for yogurt's role in gut health is extensive and well-established. The live cultures in yogurt increase microbiome diversity — a key marker of gut and overall health — and produce short-chain fatty acids (including butyrate) that nourish the intestinal epithelium, reduce gut permeability, and modulate immune function. A 2021 randomised trial published in Cell found that a high-fermented food diet (including yogurt, kefir, and kimchi) increased microbiome diversity and reduced markers of systemic inflammation more effectively than a high-fibre diet over a 10-week period, highlighting fermented dairy as a particularly potent microbiome modulator.

Yogurt reliably improves lactose tolerance. The live bacteria in yogurt produce beta-galactosidase, which breaks down lactose in the gut even in individuals with primary lactase deficiency. Controlled trials show that most lactase-deficient individuals can consume up to 240 g of yogurt daily without significant symptoms, whereas equivalent amounts of fluid milk cause bloating, cramping, and diarrhoea. This makes yogurt one of the most practical dairy foods for the estimated 65% of adults worldwide with some degree of lactase deficiency.

For bone health, the combination of calcium, phosphorus, and protein in yogurt makes it one of the most effective whole-food sources of bone-protective nutrition. The Nurses' Health Study and the Health Professionals Follow-Up Study both found that regular yogurt consumption was independently associated with lower risk of hip fracture in older adults, beyond what could be explained by calcium content alone — suggesting the protein and probiotic components also play a protective role.

Emerging evidence links yogurt consumption to reduced risk of type 2 diabetes. A 2021 meta-analysis of 22 prospective studies found that each additional 80 g serving of yogurt per day was associated with a 7% lower relative risk of type 2 diabetes, independent of other dietary factors. The proposed mechanisms include the low glycaemic index of yogurt, the metabolic effects of its probiotic bacteria on insulin sensitivity, and the anti-inflammatory effects of its specific fatty acid profile.

## How to Use

- **Choose plain, unsweetened yogurt:** Flavoured yogurts often contain as much added sugar as soft drinks. Plain yogurt provides all the nutritional benefits without the glucose and insulin spike. Sweeten naturally with fresh berries, a drizzle of honey, or a teaspoon of nut butter if desired.
- **Greek yogurt for protein-focused meals:** Strained Greek yogurt with 15–20 g of protein per serving is an outstanding high-protein breakfast or snack, particularly when paired with berries and nuts. It can also replace sour cream and mayonnaise in recipes, reducing saturated fat while adding protein.
- **Use as a cooking ingredient:** Yogurt is an excellent marinade base for chicken, lamb, and fish — the lactic acid tenderises meat while the cultures add flavour. Use it in Indian tandoori marinades, Turkish-style dips (cacık/tzatziki), and South Asian raitas (cucumber and mint condiments).
- **Make labneh:** Line a strainer with muslin cloth, add yogurt, and let it drain in the refrigerator for 24 hours. The resulting thick, cream-cheese-like labneh is extraordinarily protein-dense and calcium-rich, works as a spread on flatbreads, and can be rolled in za'atar and olive oil for a mezze dish.
- **Check for live cultures on the label:** Look for the "Live and Active Cultures" seal or a specific listing of bacterial strains on the label. Pasteurised-after-fermentation yogurt has no live cultures and provides none of the probiotic benefits. Store yogurt refrigerated and consume within the use-by date, as live culture counts decline over time.
