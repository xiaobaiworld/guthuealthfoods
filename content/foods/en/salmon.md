## Overview

Salmon is an anadromous fish species spanning several genera — most notably Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) and Pacific species including Chinook, Sockeye, Coho, and Pink salmon (Oncorhynchus spp.) — that migrate between freshwater and marine environments. It is globally recognized as one of the most nutritionally valuable foods available, delivering an exceptional concentration of long-chain omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA), complete protein, and a distinctive pink-to-red carotenoid antioxidant called astaxanthin. Regular salmon consumption is backed by some of the strongest evidence in nutritional epidemiology for benefits to cardiovascular health, brain function, and inflammation management.

## Nutritional Highlights

- **Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA)** — A 100 g serving of Atlantic salmon provides approximately 2.3 g of combined EPA and DHA, making it one of the single richest dietary sources; these fatty acids are essential for brain structure, heart health, and the resolution of inflammation.
- **Complete protein** — Provides approximately 20–25 g of high-quality protein per 100 g, with all essential amino acids in proportions well-suited to human metabolic needs.
- **Astaxanthin** — A ketocarotenoid responsible for salmon's characteristic pink color; 3–4 mg per serving in wild salmon, with potent antioxidant activity 550 times stronger than vitamin E in certain assays; linked to skin health, eye protection, and cardiovascular benefits.
- **Vitamin D** — One of very few dietary sources; a 100 g serving provides 50–80% of the daily recommended intake, supporting bone health, immune function, and mood.
- **Vitamin B12** — Provides over 100% of the daily recommended intake per serving; essential for neurological function, DNA synthesis, and red blood cell formation.
- **Selenium** — A trace mineral critical for thyroid hormone metabolism and antioxidant enzyme function; salmon is a particularly rich source.

## Health Benefits

The EPA and DHA in salmon are the two omega-3 fatty acids with the most robust clinical evidence for cardiovascular protection. They reduce serum triglycerides by up to 30% at therapeutic doses, lower blood pressure moderately, reduce platelet aggregation (making the blood less prone to clotting), and improve endothelial function. Large prospective studies consistently show that people who eat fatty fish two or more times per week have significantly lower rates of fatal cardiac events. The American Heart Association recommends two servings of fatty fish per week specifically on the basis of omega-3 research.

DHA is the most abundant omega-3 fatty acid in the brain, constituting a significant portion of the fat in neuronal cell membranes. Adequate DHA intake during pregnancy is essential for fetal brain and eye development, and in adulthood it is associated with lower risk of cognitive decline, depression, and Alzheimer's disease. Meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials show that omega-3 supplementation produces modest but consistent improvements in depression symptoms, and observational data consistently links higher fish consumption with better cognitive aging outcomes.

Astaxanthin in salmon is one of the most powerful antioxidants found in any food. Unlike many antioxidants that are limited to specific cellular compartments, astaxanthin spans the entire cell membrane and is soluble in both water and fat, giving it broad protective reach. Clinical trials show astaxanthin supplementation reduces markers of oxidative stress and inflammation, improves blood rheology (the flow properties of blood), and may improve exercise recovery. Research also supports benefits for skin elasticity and UV protection when consumed at dietary levels.

Salmon is one of the most reliable dietary sources of vitamin D, a nutrient in which deficiency is pandemic globally. Vitamin D3 from salmon is the same form produced in human skin upon sun exposure, and it is converted more efficiently to active calcitriol than the D2 form found in plants and fungi. Regular salmon consumption contributes meaningfully to vitamin D status year-round, particularly important in northern latitudes with limited sun exposure.

## How to Use

- **Wild vs. farmed** — Wild salmon generally contains higher astaxanthin levels (from natural krill-based diet) and slightly lower total fat, while farmed salmon typically provides higher omega-3 content due to controlled feed. Both are nutritionally excellent. Look for farmed salmon certified by the Aquaculture Stewardship Council (ASC) for sustainability assurance.
- **Avoid overcooking** — Salmon is best cooked to an internal temperature of 52–55°C (125–130°F) for a moist, silky texture. Overcooking to well-done degrades the omega-3 fats and albumin protein, producing the white substance that oozes out at higher temperatures. Use a thermometer for precision.
- **Simple preparations preserve nutrients** — Baking, steaming, and poaching best preserve the delicate omega-3 fatty acids and water-soluble vitamins. Pan-searing at moderate heat (not smoking oil) also works well. Avoid deep-frying, which adds unhealthy fats and oxidizes the beneficial ones.
- **Use the skin and bones** — Salmon skin is rich in omega-3s and crisps beautifully when pan-seared. Canned salmon with soft edible bones provides an excellent and affordable source of calcium alongside all the protein and omega-3 benefits.
- **Pair with vitamin C-rich sides** — While salmon's heme iron is highly bioavailable regardless of pairing, serving it alongside vegetables like broccoli, bell peppers, or citrus maximizes absorption of any non-heme iron and provides additional antioxidant protection during digestion.
