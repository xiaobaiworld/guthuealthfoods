## Overview

Pumpkin seeds (pepitas) are the edible seeds of pumpkin and squash varieties, particularly Cucurbita pepo. They have been cultivated in Mesoamerica for thousands of years and remain a staple in Mexican and Central American cuisines. Pumpkin seeds are remarkable for their density of nutrients in a small serving — just 28 g (one ounce) provides substantial amounts of magnesium, zinc, iron, and healthy unsaturated fats, along with tryptophan and a range of antioxidants. Both the green hulled seeds (commonly sold as pepitas) and the white-hulled whole seeds are nutritious.

## Nutritional Highlights

- **Magnesium** — A one-ounce serving provides approximately 37% of the daily recommended intake; magnesium supports over 300 enzymatic reactions including energy production, blood sugar regulation, and muscle and nerve function.
- **Zinc** — One of the richest plant sources of zinc, providing around 20% of daily needs per ounce; zinc is essential for immune function, wound healing, testosterone production, and taste and smell.
- **Tryptophan** — An essential amino acid that serves as a precursor to serotonin and melatonin, linking pumpkin seed consumption to mood regulation and sleep quality.
- **Healthy fats** — About 49% of pumpkin seeds' fat is polyunsaturated (mainly linoleic acid) and 34% is monounsaturated oleic acid, with minimal saturated fat.
- **Iron** — Provides around 8–11 mg per 100 g, among the highest of any seed; pairing with vitamin C-rich foods maximizes non-heme iron absorption.
- **Cucurbitacins and phytosterols** — Bioactive compounds in pumpkin seeds with anti-inflammatory and cholesterol-modulating properties; cucurbitacins are also being researched for anti-tumor activity.

## Health Benefits

Pumpkin seeds are one of the most magnesium-dense foods available, and magnesium deficiency is remarkably common in modern populations — estimates suggest 50–80% of people in developed countries consume less than the recommended daily intake. Adequate magnesium intake is associated with lower blood pressure, reduced risk of type 2 diabetes (through improved insulin sensitivity), better sleep quality, and reduced frequency of migraines. Incorporating pumpkin seeds into the diet is one of the most practical and palatable ways to address this widespread deficit.

The zinc content of pumpkin seeds is particularly relevant for immune function and hormonal health. Zinc supports the development and activation of T-cells and natural killer cells, making it essential for fighting infections. In men, zinc is concentrated in the prostate gland, and several studies suggest that pumpkin seed oil may benefit prostate health — with research supporting reduced symptoms of benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). A randomized controlled trial found that pumpkin seed oil supplementation over 12 months significantly improved urinary symptoms associated with BPH.

Tryptophan in pumpkin seeds is converted in the body to 5-HTP and then to serotonin, which can further be converted to melatonin in the pineal gland. Small studies suggest that pumpkin seed consumption alongside a carbohydrate source at night may improve sleep quality and duration, though larger human trials are needed. The combination of tryptophan, zinc, and magnesium — all present in pumpkin seeds — supports multiple aspects of healthy sleep regulation.

Pumpkin seed oil is also rich in antioxidants, including carotenoids and tocopherols, and has demonstrated anti-inflammatory effects in animal studies. Its phytosterol content helps reduce cholesterol absorption in the gut, contributing to the cardiovascular benefits associated with regular seed consumption.

## How to Use

- **Eat raw or lightly toasted** — Raw pumpkin seeds retain the highest nutrient levels. Lightly toasting in a dry pan for 5–8 minutes enhances flavor without significantly degrading nutrients, though high heat reduces some heat-sensitive antioxidants.
- **Use as a salad and grain bowl topper** — A small handful of pumpkin seeds adds crunch, protein, and nutrition to salads, grain bowls, soups, and roasted vegetable dishes. Their mild, nutty flavor is versatile enough to work in both sweet and savory contexts.
- **Blend into sauces and pesto** — Pumpkin seeds can replace pine nuts in pesto, or be blended with roasted chili peppers, garlic, and lime juice into a traditional Mexican pipian or mole verde sauce.
- **Snack on them as-is** — A one-ounce portion of unsalted pumpkin seeds makes an excellent between-meal snack, providing sustained energy from protein and fat without triggering a blood sugar spike.
- **Store properly** — Due to their high fat content, pumpkin seeds go rancid relatively quickly once opened. Store in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to three months, or freeze for up to six months.
